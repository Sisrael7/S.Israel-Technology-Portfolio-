## Reflection

**How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating "user stories" help with this?**
I interpret user needs by focusing on what the customer is actually trying to accomplish, not just
the features they ask for. On SNHU Travel, that meant asking what would help travelers find and
book trendy vacation packages and what would grow the client's customer base. User stories make
that concrete because each one is written from the user's point of view — "as a user, I want to
filter packages by budget so I can plan a trip I can afford." That format keeps the work tied to a
real person and a real goal, and it breaks a big request into small pieces the team can build,
test, and prioritize. It also gives the Product Owner a clear way to rank the backlog by value.

**How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?**
I approach development in small, iterative steps instead of trying to build everything at once. I
plan the work, build a piece, get feedback, and adjust. From this project, the Agile processes I
want to carry forward are sprint planning, keeping a prioritized backlog, short stand-ups to clear
blockers fast, and sprint reviews and retrospectives so the team keeps improving. The biggest
lesson was how well Agile handled change — when the client shifted direction, the framework let us
re-prioritize without derailing the whole project, which is something I want in my future work.

**What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?**
A good team member owns their work and their estimates, communicates early when something is
blocking them, and respects the roles around them instead of trying to do everyone's job. On this
project the roles worked precisely because each person stayed in their lane — the Product Owner
spoke for the customer, the Scrum Master cleared the road, and the developers owned the build and
sized their own work. Being a good teammate also means being honest in retrospectives about what
did and didn't work, so the team gets better every sprint.
